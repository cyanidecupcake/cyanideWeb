about jess.


Jess Weichler is a media arts educator hailing from Pittsburgh, PA (USA). Since 2009 she has worked with students ages five to eighteen, in both formal and informal classroom settings. Not content to teach just one age group, Jess is also experienced in training teachers in the fields of video editing, video game design, and stop-motion animation. She loves working alongside others to discover new ways to implement Making into classrooms


Jess enjoys playing with various media: webdesign, robotics, and photography to name a few. On this site you will find photographs, websites she maintains and designs, and her personal blog which features her adventures in education, cooking, and travel.


about conductive paintings.


"love letter to Pittsburgh"     2015

Made with electronics, code, and conductive paint this artwork is a love letter to Pittsburgh, where rivers converge and technology reigns.



Jess developed the idea of creating interactive paintings in 2014 after purchasing far too much conductive paint for a class. With the leftovers she created her first conductive painting which consisted of 2D musical instruments that could be played by the viewer.


To activate a conductive painting viewers must become a living circuit: Placing one hand on the painted hand representing "ground" while at the same time touching other parts of the painting. This completes a circuit and sends an electrical pulse to the programme that plays each sound.


For a more collaborative approach viewers can join hands with a friend to see if the electricity will still flow.


Conductive paintings are much more than art, they also teach the basics of electricity in a tangible and fun way.


credit.

Conductive paintings are the creation of Jess Weichler. She designs each one from scratch then paints them on canvas using acrylic and conductive paint, tissue paper, and wire.

Her partner, Klaatu, then attaches the wires to a MaKey MaKey which allows the painting to register each "touch". The MaKey MaKey is then hooked up to a Raspberry Pi running a custom made Python programme.

The box housing all the electronic components was imagined by Jess then perfected and laser cut by Wolf Hatch of the Wellington Makerspace in Wellington, NZ.

Many of the sounds used are from freesound.org, an Open Source, Creative Commons sound library. Others are entirely original, created by Klaatu and Jess using the Open Source programme Audacity, Qtractor, and the CALF effect plugins.
